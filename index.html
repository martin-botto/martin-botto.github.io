The question

Project Harbor models a hypothetical Salesforce acquisition of HubSpot at $300 per share. The brief was to produce the kind of analysis a senior buy-side advisory team would prepare for a board: a clear recommendation supported by independent valuation methods, a synergy build, a financing structure, an EPS impact, and an honest risk register. The whole thing is a portfolio exercise, not a real transaction. Every number cited in the 20-page memo is reproducible in the accompanying Excel model.

Four methods, in plain language

Valuation triangulates four lenses. Public trading comparables benchmark HubSpot against twelve peer SaaS companies on revenue multiples adjusted for growth. Precedent transactions pull seven completed software acquisitions between 2019 and 2025 and run a regression of multiple against target growth rate. The discounted cash flow projects eight years of free cash flow at a 10 percent WACC and a 4 percent terminal growth rate, treating stock-based compensation as a real cash cost rather than an add-back. A premiums-paid analysis cross-checks the offer against historical premia in comparable deals. Each method has limitations, which is why all four matter.

The recommendation

The model lands at $300 per share, paid 70 percent cash and 30 percent stock, valuing HubSpot at $15.7 billion. The price sits below the DCF fair value of $322, below the comparable-company range of $320 to $462, and well below the $633 per share implied by the precedent-transaction regression at HubSpot's growth rate. Year-three synergies of $1.44 billion produce roughly $10 billion in net value creation after the premium paid, with one-time integration costs and a year of EPS dilution before accretion arrives in year two.

What this project demonstrates

Project Harbor is one demonstration of analytical capability, not the defining one. It shows modeling discipline through a fully linked Excel that reproduces every figure cited in the memo, including the sensitivity grid and synergy build. It shows written communication through a 20-page document that explains every finance term inline, footnotes its sources, and stays readable for someone who is not an analyst. And it shows end-to-end analytical work: defining the question, building the structure, defending the numbers, and writing the recommendation for a busy reader. The same approach scales to operations dashboards, BI reporting, and any domain where data needs to drive a decision.
